Conceptual Introduction

Perhaps the primary goal of condensed matter physics is to classify phases of matter. Historically, the best tool in that pursuit has been symmetry (see Landau's theory of second order phase transitions), but more recently topology has emerged as a critical tool for understanding quantum matter. In particular, topology has proven to be highly fruitful in studying crystalline systems where the theory largely goes by the name topological band theory. A proper introduction to the subject requires quite a bit of background and can come from a variety of perspectives, but the gist is that a topological phase is one which exhibits some form of topological obstruction (twist, hole, etc.) that separates it from the vacuum. This twist or hole is not in real space but instead is in the state space (i.e. it is a property of the groundstate) making it fairly abstract to fresh eyes.

A central feature of topological phases is that they tend to have unusual surface properties. Intuitively, since there is a "twist" in the bulk of a topological solid and there is no twist in the vacuum/air outside, there must be some sort of "untwisting" at the surface. The result is bizarre surface behavior that is highly robust and is said to be topologically protected (at least perturbatively). This was the focus of my research — topological surface states.

One approach known as topological quantum chemistry uses graph theory and an unorthodox representation theory of space groups to infer if a band structure has a corresponding real space description. Here, a topological system is one which does not have such a real space description and is said to have an obstructed atomic limit.

Another more general approach uses topological K-theory (pdf), a scheme for classifying vector bundles by their topological properties. Here, we utilize the fact that the ground state configuration of a crystalline system forms a vector bundle over a compact momentum space which is isomorphic to a d-torus in d dimensions.

LatticeDynamics.jl

As part of my graduate work studying topological surface phonons, I developed a Julia package called LatticeDynamics.jl. It consists of tools for studying crystal phonons by building interatomic force models and computing their bulk and surface phonon structure. Surfaces can be studied in both slab and semi-infinite geometries.
